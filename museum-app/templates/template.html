Statistical Analysis and Unsupervised Clustering of Asian Art in American Museums

Jenny Sheng
Princeton University Class of 2022
Advised by Professor Brian Kernighan

Within the past five years, there has been an increased interest in the idea of collections as data. Collections as data is this concept of treating cultural heritage collections as datasets that can also be examined statistically and computationally. This is part of a movement to expand the spheres of cultural heritage analysis from careful scrutinization to large scale computation and research. In response to this idea, museums have also made much effort in making their collections database publicly available in bulk. For instance, the Getty adopted an Open Content Program in order to make their artwork images available to researchers. Numerous other museums have started to make their data be open access to the public under the Creative Common Zero license (CC0).

However, as these changes take place and museum collections become increasingly accessible, there have been surprisingly few efforts and attempts to examine these data together as a whole. Museums represent the cultural landscapes of the world. They are not only windows into the past and present, but also tell interesting tales of curatorial trends, public interest in specific spheres of art, cultural representation, provenance representation, etc. Yet, none of these trends can be confidently examined just by considering one specific collection. There is definitely more to the story when all these disparate museum collections are merged together to create a more holistic view.

The goal of our project is to examine the intersection of the Asian art sphere between different museums, and find commonalities and differences across disparate museum collections in order to gain a better understanding of the Asian art collection in the United States. This goal is achieved in two steps. First, through rectification and merging of museum metadata, we aim to explore statistical relationships between museum object acquisition across the curatorial landscape. Second, through exploration of museum images, we aim to cluster images and explore the visual similarities of artworks across datasets. Furthermore, a subsidiary goal of this project is to design a process for rectifying museum collections that can be used for reference in future works related to the intersectionality of museum collections. We examine the following five museum datasets: Princeton University Art Museum, The Metropolitan Museum of Art, Modern Museum of Art, Carnegie Museum of Art, and Minneapolis Institution of Art.

This project is a Spring 2021 independent work. Special thanks to Professor Brian Kernighan, Dr. Zoe LeBlanc, Vivien Nguyen, and my classmates in IW06 seminar for their help and support throughout this independent work.

Please find the details of this project at https://github.com/jsheng112/asian-art-in-american-museums
